Mercury:

Mercury is the first planet from the Sun and the smallest planet in the Solar System. It is also the closest planet to the Sun, orbiting at an average distance of about 58 million kilometers (36 million miles). Because it is so close to the Sun, Mercury experiences extreme temperatures, ranging from about 430°C (800°F) during the day to -180°C (-290°F) at night. Unlike Earth, Mercury has a very thin atmosphere, called an exosphere, which cannot trap heat effectively.

Mercury travels around the Sun faster than any other planet, completing one orbit in just 88 Earth days. However, it rotates slowly on its axis, so one day on Mercury lasts about 59 Earth days. The planet has no moons or rings. Its surface is heavily covered with craters, similar to Earth's Moon, because it has been struck by many asteroids and meteoroids over billions of years. Scientists have studied Mercury using spacecraft, which have revealed cliffs, plains, and large impact basins across its rocky surface.

Mercury is named after the Roman messenger god, who was known for his speed. The planet was given this name because it moves quickly across the sky compared to the other planets. Although Mercury is difficult to see because it stays close to the Sun, it has been observed by people since ancient times and remains an important object of study for astronomers today.
